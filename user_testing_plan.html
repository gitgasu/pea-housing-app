People Empowering App

User Testing Plan & Protocol - Version 1.0
Testing Goal: Validate that the PEA prototype delivers on its core promise: making housing navigation simple, supportive, and accessible for NYC residents facing housing insecurity.

1. Testing Overview

Research Questions

  1. Usability: Can users complete core tasks without confusion or frustration?
  2. Value Proposition: Do users find the app helpful compared to current methods?
  3. Feature Validation: Do the validated features (action plans, human support, proactive suggestions) work as intended?
  4. Accessibility: Can users with varying tech comfort levels navigate successfully?
  5. Emotional Response: Does the app feel supportive rather than overwhelming?

4. Test Scenarios & Tasks

Think-Aloud Protocol: Remind participants to verbalize their thoughts as they complete tasks. Say: "Please think out loud as you go - tell me what you're looking for, what you're thinking, what confuses you or makes sense."

Task 1: First Impressions & Onboarding

1Task: Open the app for the first time and get started

Scenario:

"You've just downloaded the People Empowering App on your phone. You need help finding housing in NYC. Open the app and see if it can help you."

Success Criteria:

  • ‚úì Understands app purpose within 15 seconds
  • ‚úì Completes onboarding without confusion
  • ‚úì Selects appropriate need category
  • ‚úì Reaches dashboard successfully

Observation Points:

  • Does user read welcome text or skip immediately?
  • Can user identify which option matches their need?
  • Does "I'm not sure ‚Üí talk to someone" option make sense?
  • Any hesitation or confusion expressed?

Follow-up Questions:

  1. "What did you think this app was for after reading the welcome screen?"
  2. "Was it clear which option to choose? Why or why not?"
  3. "Did anything confuse you or make you unsure?"

Moderator Note: This tests the core "Don't make me more confused" principle. If users express confusion here, we've failed immediately.

Task 2: Finding the Help Button

2Task: Get help from a real person

Scenario:

"You're on the dashboard and you have a question. You want to talk to someone who can help you. How would you do that?"

Success Criteria:

  • ‚úì Finds help button within 10 seconds
  • ‚úì Understands multiple support channels available
  • ‚úì Can identify which channel works best for them

Observation Points:

  • Do they find the help button immediately or search around?
  • Is the yellow "Get Help" button visible enough?
  • Do they understand chat vs. phone vs. video options?
  • Which support channel would they choose and why?

Follow-up Questions:

  1. "Was it easy or hard to find how to get help?"
  2. "Which way would you prefer to get help? Why?"
  3. "Do you feel confident you could reach a real person if you needed to?"

Moderator Note: 100% of research participants wanted human support option. This tests visibility and accessibility of this critical feature.

Task 3: Understanding Action Plan

3Task: Navigate the step-by-step action plan

Scenario:

"You're on Step 2 of your housing application. Look at the action plan and tell me what you need to do next."

Success Criteria:

  • ‚úì Identifies current step clearly
  • ‚úì Understands what documents are needed
  • ‚úì Knows how to proceed if stuck or missing something
  • ‚úì Recognizes progress made (completed steps)

Observation Points:

  • Can they articulate what Step 2 requires without re-reading?
  • Do they notice the community tip? Do they trust it?
  • Do they see the "I'm missing a document" button?
  • Does progress bar make sense to them?

Follow-up Questions:

  1. "In your own words, what do you need to do for Step 2?"
  2. "How do you feel about the community tip from other users?"
  3. "If you were missing one of the documents, what would you do?"
  4. "Does seeing Step 1 marked as complete make you feel any particular way?"

Moderator Note: Step-by-step plans were rated valuable by 100% of participants. This tests if the execution matches the need.

Task 4: Searching for Housing

4Task: Find a no-fee apartment that accepts vouchers

Scenario (Ms. Ellis / Mr. Patterson types):

"You're looking for a 1-bedroom apartment in your price range with no broker fee. Try to find some options."

Success Criteria:

  • ‚úì Navigates to search from dashboard
  • ‚úì Uses filters to refine results
  • ‚úì Identifies no-fee listings
  • ‚úì Understands listing information (price, features, landlord rating)

Observation Points:

  • Do they notice "No broker fee" is already selected?
  • Do they add "Accepts vouchers" filter if needed?
  • Can they explain what the landlord rating means?
  • Do they click into a listing or stay on results?

Follow-up Questions:

  1. "How easy or hard was it to find apartments you were looking for?"
  2. "What information was most important to you in the search results?"
  3. "Did you notice anything about broker fees? What did you think?"
  4. "How do you feel about the landlord ratings shown?"

Moderator Note: Mr. Patterson's #1 priority was avoiding broker fees. Test if no-fee filter prominence is sufficient.

Task 5: Landlord Information (Ms. Ellis types only)

5Task: Check if a landlord accepts vouchers

Scenario:

"You have a housing voucher. Before contacting a landlord, you want to make sure they actually accept vouchers and have a good track record. How can you find this out?"

Success Criteria:

  • ‚úì Finds landlord profile/info section
  • ‚úì Identifies voucher acceptance rate
  • ‚úì Reads community reviews from other voucher holders
  • ‚úì Feels confident about landlord before contacting

Observation Points:

  • Do they find the "Landlord" tab or section?
  • Do they notice the 95% voucher acceptance rate?
  • Do they read the voucher-specific reviews?
  • Does the "Voucher-Friendly Landlord" badge stand out?

Follow-up Questions:

  1. "Does this information help you feel more confident about contacting this landlord?"
  2. "How does this compare to how you normally find out if a landlord accepts vouchers?"
  3. "Would you trust this information? Why or why not?"

Moderator Note: Ms. Ellis's #1 request: landlords to have information before meeting. This is the differentiating feature.

Task 6: Document Management

6Task: Upload a required document

Scenario:

"You need to upload your photo ID for your housing application. Show me how you would do that."

Success Criteria:

  • ‚úì Navigates to document section
  • ‚úì Understands upload options (photo, files, cloud)
  • ‚úì Finds help if document is missing
  • ‚úì Sees progress toward completion

Observation Points:

  • Do they try "Take Photo Now" first (mobile-native)?
  • Do they notice the progress indicator (4 of 6 complete)?
  • Do they see the "Don't have this document?" help section?
  • Does the green/yellow color coding make sense?

Follow-up Questions:

  1. "Which upload method would you use in real life? Why?"
  2. "Does the progress tracker (4 of 6) help or not? How?"
  3. "If you were missing a document, would you know what to do?"

Moderator Note: 33% cited missing documents as challenge; 50% use photo features. Test if solution addresses both issues.

Task 7: Open Exploration

7Task: Free exploration and feedback

Instruction:

"You can explore the app however you'd like for the next few minutes. Click around, try things, and tell me what you notice."

Observation Points:

  • What do they click on first?
  • Do they revisit the help button?
  • Do they explore features not covered in tasks?
  • What do they comment on positively or negatively?

Follow-up Questions:

  1. "What stood out to you most about the app?"
  2. "Was there anything you expected to find but didn't?"
  3. "Was there anything that surprised you or seemed unnecessary?"

5. Moderator Script

Introduction (5 minutes)

Moderator: "Hi [Name], thank you so much for coming today. I'm [Your Name] and I'll be guiding you through this session.

We're testing a prototype of an app designed to help people find housing in New York City. I want to be really clear: we're testing the app, not you. There are no right or wrong answers. If something doesn't make sense or is hard to use, that's really valuable feedback for us.

What I'd like you to do is use the app like you normally would, and as you go, please think out loud. Tell me what you're looking at, what you're thinking, what you expect to happen. If you get confused or frustrated, that's exactly what we need to know.

I'm going to be taking notes and [recording/observing], and everything you say will be kept confidential. Your name won't be attached to any of our findings.

The session will take about an hour. At the end, I'll give you a $50 gift card as a thank you for your time.

Do you have any questions before we begin?"

[Wait for questions, answer honestly. Have participant sign consent form and start recording if approved.]

Background Questions (5 minutes)

Moderator: "Before we look at the app, I'd like to ask you a few background questions."

  1. "Have you had to search for housing in NYC in the past few years? Can you tell me a bit about that experience?"

    [Listen for pain points, frustrations, what worked/didn't work]

  2. "When you were searching, what resources did you use? Websites, apps, calling places, word of mouth?"

    [Note their current methods for comparison later]

  3. "How comfortable are you with using apps on your phone in general?"

    [Gauge tech comfort to contextualize observations]

  4. "If you could wave a magic wand and have the perfect tool to help you find housing, what would it do?"

    [Captures unmet needs and expectations]

Task Introduction

Moderator: "Great, thank you. Now I'm going to give you some scenarios to try with the app. Remember, think out loud as you go. I might stay quiet while you work - that's normal, I'm just watching and taking notes. If you get really stuck, I'll step in, but I want to see how you'd handle it on your own first."

[Hand them the device with prototype loaded to home screen]

Moderator: "Okay, here's your first scenario..." [Begin Task 1]

During Tasks - Probing Questions

Use these probes when participant is silent or unclear:

  • "What are you thinking right now?"
  • "What are you looking for?"
  • "What do you expect to happen when you tap that?"
  • "Can you walk me through what you're seeing here?"
  • "How do you feel about that?" [after they complete something]

If stuck for 30+ seconds:

  • "What would you try next?"
  • "If this were the real app, what would you do right now?"
  • [If still stuck after 1 minute]: "Let me give you a hint..." [minimal guidance]

Post-Task Interview (10 minutes)

Moderator: "Thank you so much for working through those tasks with me. Now I have some overall questions about your experience."

Overall Impressions:

  1. "Thinking about the whole app, what's your overall impression?"
  2. "Would you use this app if it were available today? Why or why not?"
  3. "Would you recommend it to a friend or family member looking for housing?"

Comparison to Current Tools:

  1. "How does this compare to [the methods you mentioned earlier - websites, 311, etc.]?"
  2. "What does this app do better? What does it do worse?"
  3. "Is there anything this app does that you wish other tools did?"

Specific Features:

  1. "What did you think about the step-by-step action plans?"
  2. "How did you feel about being able to chat with a real person for help?"
  3. "What about the community tips from other users - helpful or not?"
  4. [For voucher holders]: "The landlord information showing voucher acceptance rates - valuable?"

What's Missing:

  1. "Was there anything you expected to see that wasn't there?"
  2. "If you could add one feature to this app, what would it be?"

Accessibility & Confusion:

  1. "Did anything confuse you or make you feel lost?"
  2. "Was there anything that made you feel frustrated?"
  3. "On a scale of 1-10, how easy was this app to use?" [Note their reasoning]

Qualitative Observations

Observation Template (fill out per task)

Analysis Framework

After Each Session:

  • Review notes immediately while fresh
  • Calculate quantitative metrics (task completion, time, errors)
  • Note any critical issues or bugs to fix before next session
  • Identify patterns emerging across participants

After All Sessions:

  1. Quantitative Analysis:
    • Calculate average task completion rates per task
    • Calculate average time on task
    • Calculate average SUS score (overall and per persona)
    • Create charts showing performance across tasks
  2. Qualitative Analysis:
    • Affinity mapping: Group similar observations, quotes, issues
    • Identify recurring themes (both positive and negative)
    • Create severity ratings for issues found:
      • Critical: Blocks task completion, causes abandonment
      • Major: Significant confusion/frustration, workarounds needed
      • Minor: Small annoyances, doesn't block progress
  3. Persona Comparison:
    • Compare performance across persona types (Mr. L vs Ms. Ellis vs Mr. Patterson)
    • Identify if certain features work better for specific personas
    • Note any unexpected findings or contradictions to initial research
  4. Recommendation Prioritization:
    • Must fix before launch (critical issues)
    • Should fix soon (major usability problems)
    • Nice to have (minor improvements)
    • Future enhancements (feature requests)

7. Success Validation

We've succeeded if:

  • Mr. L types never say "I'm confused" during onboarding or action plans
  • Ms. Ellis types feel the landlord info is valuable and builds confidence
  • Mr. Patterson types immediately notice no-fee filter and cost savings
  • 100% can find human support within 10 seconds
  • 80%+ complete tasks without moderator intervention
  • SUS score ‚â•70 (above average usability)
  • Participants say they'd use it over current methods

Red flags to watch for:

  • Multiple participants saying "This is confusing" or "I don't know what to do"
  • Task completion rate <60%
  • Participants abandon tasks out of frustration
  • Tech-uncomfortable users struggle significantly more than comfortable users
  • Help button isn't found when needed
  • Participants prefer their current methods over the app
  • SUS score <50 (poor usability)

10. Ethical Considerations

Trauma-Informed Research Practices

  • Participant autonomy: Emphasize they can skip questions, stop anytime, no penalty
  • Privacy protection: All data anonymized, no names in reports
  • Comfortable environment: Provide water, snacks, breaks as needed
  • Respectful language: No judgment about housing situation or tech skills
  • Immediate compensation: Don't make participants wait for payment
  • No deception: Be honest about research purpose and use of data
  • Sensitivity to crisis: If participant reveals immediate housing crisis, provide 311/DHS resources
